How to Make Your School Paper More Interesting



There are plenty characteristics of a good essay: original thinking, a tight structure, balanced arguments, and some more. However, one aspect often overlooked is that a good essay ought to be interesting. It should spark the interest of the readers, keep them absorbed, make them want to continue to reading and learn more. A boring essay increases the chances of losing the reading's attention; regardless of whether the points you make are excellent, a dull writing style or poor handling of a dry subject matter can undermine the positive aspects of the essay. The issue or problem is that numerous students thought that essays ought to be this way: they imagine that a dull, dry style is fit to the purposes of academic writing, and don't consider that the teacher reading their paper needs to find the essay interesting. Academic writing doesn't need to be – and shouldn't be – boring. Fortunately there are a lot of things you can do to make your writing more interesting, despite the fact that you can only do a lot while remaining within the formal confines of academic writing. Let's take a look at what they are.

1. Be interested in what you’re writing about

If there's one thing guaranteed to inject interest into your writing, it's actually being interested on the thing you're writing about. Passion for a subject comes across naturally in your composition, typically making it all the more lively and engaging, and mixing an infectious enthusiasm into your words – similarly that it's not difficult to chat knowledgeably to someone about something you find interesting. This makes it relatively easy to write interestingly regarding a matter you have a real passion for. Nonetheless, problems emerge when you're forced to write a paper about subjects for which you lack enthusiasm. It's hard to invoke passion for your least favorite subjects, and that will come across in your writing. There are steps you can take, however: here are a few tips on writing about a subject you least enjoy.

  • Adjust your mindset: convince yourself that there are no boring subjects. If the subject or essay seems to be boring, the fault is in yourself; If you wind up feeling negatively about it, try to discover the interest in it. Consider how it relates to the reality and how significant the subject is. Find interesting snippets of information about it and take a look at it from another point.
  • Think about your reader: consider the fact that not every person will find the subject as boring as you do. As you write, remember the reader and imagine them to be the world's greatest enthusiast of this subject.
  • Find the fans: if you think that its not possible to get into the mindset of your audience, try Googling the subject to find forums, videos or blog post in which the subject is talked about by individuals who do think that its interesting. This will help you picture whom you're writing for, and give you an alternate point of view regarding a subject you might not have found inspiring to now.

2. Include fascinating details

Another factor that can make an essay boring is a dry topic. A few subjects or topic areas are naturally dry, and it falls to you to make the essay more interesting through your writing style and by attempting to discover fascinating snippets of information to include that will liven it up a bit and make the information simpler to relate with. One method of doing this with a dry subject is to try to make what you're talking about seem to be relevant to the reality, as this is easier for the reader to relate with. In a discussion of an apparently boring piece of legislation, for example, you could comment something in accordance with the lines of "if it were not for this legislation, none of us would enjoy the freedom to do such today", or "Legislation A ultimately paved the way for Legislation B, which transformed criminal law as far as we know it." Make it appear to be exciting!

3. Emulate the style of writers you find interesting

When you read a lot, you subconsciously begin emulating the style of the writers you read. It's therefore helpful to read widely, as this opens you to a range of styles and you can start to take on the characteristics of those you find interesting to read. If you feel engaged with a piece of writing, the writer must be something right! As you read, think how the writer is doing hold your interest, maybe underlining or copying certain phrases, techniques, sentence structures, and so on. Then at that point apply their techniques to your own writing.

4. Write in the active voice

It's the oldest trick in the book, but using the active rather than the passive voice will naturally make your writing more interesting to read. It results in more straightforward, energetic writing that causes the reader to feel more 'in the moment'. Sadly, numerous students employ the passive voice in the belief that it makes their writing sound more academic or intellectual; when in fact, it makes their writing sound boring. Keep in mind, the active voice is the point at which the subject of the sentence "acts", while the passive voice is the point at which the subject is acted upon.
Passive: It was concluded by the scientist that the method used were…
Active: The scientist concluded that the method used were…
The subject in this example is "the scientist" and the "act" they are carrying out is "concluding". As you can see in this example, the active voice quite often brings about neater and more elegant phrasing, which is more concise and enjoyable to read.

5. Borrow some creative writing techniques

There's clearly a limit to the amount of actual 'story-telling' you can do when you're writing a paper; all things considered, essays ought to be objective, factual and balanced, which doesn't, at the first look, feel very much like story-telling. However, you can apply some of the principles of story-telling to make your more interesting. For instance, similarly as the opening sentence or paragraph section of a novel is incredibly important in catching the attention of the reader right off the bat, so the first paragraph of your essay is essential in making your reader want to keep reading it. Start with an attention grabbing 'hook' to draw them, like a controversial statement, an enticing bit of information or a rhetorical question Here are some more techniques you can adopt from creative writing to improve your essays.

6. Think about your own opinion

Your essay is bound to be boring if everything you do is paraphrase what every other person says about something. A good essay – in humanities subjects, at least – includes the writer's intelligent responses to what others say, and this critical consideration not just shows that you're thinking at a high academic level, but it automatically adds more interest and originality to your writing. Along these lines, think independently and don't be afraid to show that you're doing as much.

7. Cut the waffle

Rambling on and on is boring, and nearly ensured to lose the interest of your reader. You're at risk of waffling If you're not totally clear about what you need to say, or if that you haven't thought how you will structure your argument. Doing your research appropriately and writing an essay plan before you start will help you prevent this problem.

Editing is an important part of the essay writing process, so whenever you've done a first draft, edit out the waffle. Read your essay objectively and take out the bits that isn't relevant to the argument or that work the point. Just as editing out chunks of text, it's important to be conservative with words – not using ten where five will do, and keeping away from clunky phrases like those illustrated in this article. During the editing process, tighten out your phrasing by eliminating of unnecessary words and reordering any sentences that read gravely.

8. Using a thesaurus isn’t always a good thing

You may think that using a thesaurus to find more complicated words will make your writing fascinating, or sound more academic, but using excessively high-brow language can have wrong effect. It alienates the reader and makes you sound pompous, with the result that the essay is more laborious to read and the reader may rapidly lose interest. Regardless of this, numerous students admit to purposely over-complicate their language to make it sound all the more high-brow. If you want to keep your reader interested, keep your language clear and straightforward.

9. Avoid repetitive phrasing

Avoid using similar sentence structure over and over: it's a recipe for dullness! instead, use a range of syntax that shows your writing abilities just as making your writing more interesting. Blend simple, compound and complex sentences to avoid from your writing getting predictable.

10. Use some figurative language

As we've already seen, it's not difficult to end up rambling when you're explaining difficult ideas or concepts, – especially when you don't clearly understand it yourself. A method of forcing yourself to think clearly about a concept, just as explaining it all the more simply and engagingly, is to utilize figurative language. This means explaining something by comparing it and something else, as in an analogy. For example, you may use the analogy of water scaping from a hole in a can to explain the exponential decay of a radioactive substance, as the rate of depletion of both relies upon how much remains, making it exponential. This gives the reader something familiar to picture, making it simpler for them to understand new concept (obviously this won't be another new concept for the instructor who set your essay, however they will need to see that you can explain concepts clearly and that you have a thorough grasp of it yourself).

11. Avoid clichés

Clichés are overused words or phrases that make your writing unsurprising, and therefore less interesting. An example would be "at the end of the day", but there are numerous such favorites of student essay-writers. Remember that your teacher will have a pile of papers to read at a time; if you use similar tired expressions every other person uses, your essay will mix in with all the others. Make it stand out by avoiding the clichés you know your classmates will be use.

12. Employ rhetorical questions

One of the ways in which ancient speakers held the attention of their audiences and increased the dramatic effect of their speeches was by using the rhetorical question. What is a rhetorical question? It's basically one you ask without expecting that your audience would answer – one that you will answer yourself, similar to the one we asked in the previous sentence. This can be an effective method of introducing another line of enquiry, or of bringing up question that you will address in more detail. A good place to use a rhetorical question is toward the finish of a paragraph, to lead into the next one, or toward the start of another paragraph to introduce another area for exploration. The rhetorical question, "But is there any evidence to support X's claim?" could, for instance, start a paragraph that discusses evidence for an opinion presented in the previous paragraph. What's more, as we've already seen, you could utilize a rhetorical question as your 'hook' to lure readers in directly toward the start of your essay.

13. Proofread

At long last, you could write the most interesting essay a teacher has ever read, however you'll undermine your great work if it's covered with errors, which distract the reader from the actual content and will presumably bother them. Before you submit your paper, proofread it thoroughly to guarantee that the grammar is rich, the punctuation is great and the spelling is flawless. Don't simply use a spelling and grammar checker, as these don't generally get on every one of the errors.
